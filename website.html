Wilkinson's Data Transformations and their Uses:
An Exploration through Real-World Examples

In Table 4.1 of Leland Wilkinson’s Grammar of Graphics, Wilkinson defines three classes of data variable transformations: mathematical, statistical, and multivariate. Although he provides a thorough list and brief explanations of these twenty-eight data transformations, Wilkinson does not provide real or synthetic examples of when and how these data transformations are applied. Furthermore, Wilkinson does not provide context for the value of these transformations in the creation of meaningful data visualizations. For this reason, we have selected a collection of these twenty-eight data transformations to study various real world data sets. In these studies, we seek to illuminate use cases of these transformations as they relate to data visualization and explore their intrinsic value in the field.

US Econ Census Data


In this section, we cover the uses of the Log, Mean, Sort, Sum, Rank, and Group data transformations on data contained in the U.S. Economic Census in year 2002, 2007, and 2012. We use this transformations to create scatter plots of industrial annual pay both generally and by state, ranked scatter plots of average annual income, and a rank swimlane visual of the states in order by revenue each year the census was taken.

Google Stock and SAT Data


In this section, we use the Lag, Difference, and Sign data transformations in the creation of a time series of Google stock prices from April 2014 to April 2015 (data taken from Yahoo Finance). The time series is color coded to display overall increase and decrease of stock price by month. We also use use the prank(), rank() and cut() data transformations to create a visualization of a distribution of SAT scores. We start with a normal distribution of 10000 scores with mean 1500 and standard deviation 330. We then color this distribution based on percentile rank of scores with the lowest percentile range being the lightest color and the highest percentile range being the darkest color.

Mortality Data


In this section, we demonstrate the use of the arcsine, median, mode, cut, quotient, residual, influence, and miss data transformations to explore the World Health Organization’s data on mortality by country in 2009 and 2010. By applying each individual transformation or a combination of transformations, we can analyze the data in different ways, pulling new information each time from visuals such as heat maps of infant mortality rates, box plots of median infant mortality rate, and frequency distributions of the death count by age.
